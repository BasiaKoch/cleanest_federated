\section{Experimental Setting}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Dataset}
We use DermaMNIST \citep{Yang et al., 2023}, the dermatology subset of the
MedMNIST~v2 benchmark, derived from the HAM10000 collection of
dermatoscopic images \citep{Tschandl et al., 2018}. DermaMNIST contains 10{,}015
dermoscopic images of skin lesions across seven diagnostic categories,
split into 7{,}007 training, 1{,}003 validation, and 2{,}005 test samples in
the canonical MedMNIST split. Images are loaded from the official
\texttt{dermamnist\_64.npz} archive at 64$\times$64 resolution and resized
to 28$\times$28 to match the MedMNIST~v1 baseline. The class distribution
is severely imbalanced, with a 58:1 ratio between the most prevalent
(\textit{melanocytic nevi}, 67.05\%) and least prevalent
(\textit{dermatofibroma}, 1.15\%) classes. This imbalance is preserved without
re-sampling so that any reported gain reflects realistic clinical priors.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
The classifier is a four-block convolutional neural network
(\texttt{DermMNISTCNN}, $\sim$423K parameters) with feature widths
$32\to 64\to 128\to 256$, each block consisting of two 3$\times$3
convolutions, GroupNorm, ReLU, and max-pooling, followed by global
average pooling and a 256\,$\to$\,128\,$\to$\,7 fully connected head with
dropout 0.2. GroupNorm \citep{Wu \& He, 2018} is used in place of
BatchNorm because per-client batch statistics encode local distribution
information that cannot be safely averaged across heterogeneous clients
\citep{Hsieh et al., 2020}.

\subsection{Federated Learning Setup}
Training is performed with the Flower 1.x simulation framework
\citep{Beutel et al., 2020}. The server orchestrates a fixed pool of seven
clients with full participation per round (fraction-fit $C = 1.0$). At
each of $R = 150$ communication rounds, the server broadcasts the global
parameter vector $w^t$ to all clients; each client performs $E = 20$
local epochs of SGD on its private partition; the server then aggregates
the updated parameters with the size-weighted FedAvg rule,
$w^{t+1} = \sum_i (n_i / \sum_j n_j)\, w_i^{t+1}$. After aggregation, the
global model is evaluated on the validation set. The test set is
evaluated once per run at the round of best validation macro-F1, in
accordance with the standard early-stopping protocol \citep{Goodfellow et al., 2016}.

Hyperparameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\subsection{Algorithms}
\textbf{FedAvg} \citep{McMahan et al., 2017} minimises the local
cross-entropy objective for $E$ epochs per round and aggregates the
resulting client parameters by the size-weighted mean above.
\textbf{FedProx} \citep{Li et al., 2020} augments the local objective with
a proximal regulariser anchoring the local model to the round-start
global parameters:
\[
\mathcal{L}_i^{\text{FedProx}}(w) = \mathcal{L}_i^{\text{FedAvg}}(w)
+ \tfrac{\mu}{2}\, \lVert w - w^t \rVert_2^2.
\]
When $\mu = 0$, FedProx reduces to FedAvg. We use $\mu = 0.01$, selected
on a three-seed pilot sweep over $\{0.001, 0.01, 0.1\}$. The proximal
anchor $w^t$ is snapshotted at the start of each round and detached from
the autograd graph; the term is summed over all trainable parameters.

\subsection{Partition: \texttt{balanced\_paired\_7\_clients}}
We use a custom non-IID partition designed to satisfy three desiderata:
(i) the global class distribution is heterogeneous across clients to
challenge FedAvg's gradient-averaging assumption; (ii) no client
dominates aggregation; (iii) no class is held by exactly one client, as
singleton ownership caused catastrophic per-class regression in pilot
experiments with an earlier ``specialist'' partition. Composition is
given in Table~\ref{tab:partition}.

\subsection{Paired-Seed Protocol}
The single most important experimental control is paired seeding: for
each seed $s \in S = \{42,\, 123,\, 456,\, 789,\, 999,\, 2024,\, 31337,\, 161803,\, 271828,\, 8675309\}$,
FedAvg and FedProx are run with identical random state---identical
partition, identical model initialisation, identical dataloader RNG.
Within-seed paired differences therefore reflect only the algorithmic
difference (presence vs.\ absence of the proximal term), not random
seed variance. Each of the 20 SLURM jobs (10 seeds $\times$ 2
algorithms) ran for approximately one to one-and-a-half hours on a
single A100; total sweep compute was $\sim$25~GPU-hours.

\subsection{Metrics and Statistical Analysis}
The primary outcome is test macro-F1, the unweighted mean of per-class
F1 scores. Accuracy is reported as a secondary descriptor but is
misleading under the severe class imbalance present in DermaMNIST.
Per-class F1 is reported separately to detect class-specific
regressions. Validation metrics are recorded after every communication
round and aggregated across the 10 paired seeds with mean and standard
error of the mean (SEM); the test set is evaluated once per run at the
round of best validation macro-F1, producing 20 single-shot test
evaluations from which all reported test statistics are computed.

The headline test macro-F1 comparison uses the paired Wilcoxon
signed-rank test \citep{Demsar, 2006}, robust to the non-normality and
small sample size ($n = 10$) of paired differences. We report the
two-sided $p$-value as the primary statistic and the rank-biserial
correlation $r_{rb}$ \citep{Kerby, 2014} as the effect size. Bootstrap
95\% confidence intervals on the mean paired difference are computed
from 10{,}000 resamples \citep{Efron \& Tibshirani, 1993}. Per-class
paired Wilcoxon tests are computed identically on the seven per-class
test F1 vectors. Macro-F1 is treated as the single primary outcome to
avoid multiple-comparison inflation; balanced accuracy and accuracy are
reported descriptively.

\subsection{Final Test Performance}
Across the 10 paired seeds, FedProx outperformed FedAvg on the primary
outcome (Table~\ref{tab:headline}). The two-sided paired Wilcoxon
signed-rank test on test macro-F1 yielded $p = 0.020$, with a
rank-biserial effect size of $r_{rb} = +0.818$
(``very large''; \citealt{Kerby, 2014}); the bootstrap 95\% confidence
interval on the mean paired difference excludes zero. FedProx
outperformed FedAvg on 9 of 10 paired seeds. The single losing seed
(789) yielded $\Delta = -0.020$, small in magnitude and consistent with
random initialisation variance.

\subsection{Per-Class Test Performance}
The class-wise breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:per-class}) localises the
macro-F1 advantage to two classes: \textit{melanoma}, with the largest
mean improvement ($\Delta = +0.114$, $p = 0.006$), and \textit{actinic
keratoses} ($\Delta = +0.067$, $p = 0.020$). These are also the two
most-represented minority classes. No class showed a statistically
significant regression: the largest mean per-class regression was on
\textit{vascular lesions} ($\Delta = -0.012$, $p = 0.70$), well within
the pre-registered safety tolerance of $\pm 0.05$. The majority class
(\textit{melanocytic nevi}, 67\% prevalence) was statistically
unchanged ($\Delta = +0.002$, $p = 0.38$), confirming that FedProx's
regularisation did not degrade performance on the dominant class.

\subsection{Convergence Behaviour}
Figure~\ref{fig:curves-main} presents the per-round validation
trajectories aggregated as mean $\pm$ SEM across the 10 paired seeds.
At round~1, both algorithms are statistically indistinguishable (mean
val macro-F1 = 0.115 for both), confirming that the paired-seed
protocol successfully equalises initial conditions. FedProx pulls
ahead within five rounds and maintains a consistent advantage
throughout training: the gap is largest near round~50 ($\Delta =
+0.094$ on validation macro-F1) and narrows to $+0.039$ by round~150
as FedAvg slowly closes the early-training difference. FedProx reaches
val macro-F1 = 0.45 in 13 rounds on average, versus 39 rounds for
FedAvg, representing an approximately threefold communication-efficiency
gain. Validation loss reaches its minimum near round~30 for both
algorithms and rises thereafter, indicating overfitting under the
chosen $E = 20$ local-epoch regime; FedProx's validation loss
nonetheless remains 0.10--0.18 below FedAvg's throughout the second
half of training. The training-loss panel (log scale) provides
mechanistic evidence that the proximal term is actively constraining
local updates: FedAvg's training loss decays to approximately 0.002 by
round~150, whereas FedProx's training loss is held above 0.004---a
2.2$\times$ gap that persists because the proximal regulariser
$\frac{\mu}{2}\lVert w - w^t \rVert_2^2$ contributes a strictly
positive penalty whenever the local model diverges from the global
anchor. Validation balanced accuracy (panel~D) closely tracks
validation macro-F1, confirming that the result is not an artefact of
the macro averaging.

\subsection{Per-Class Convergence}
Figure~\ref{fig:curves-per-class} shows the per-class validation F1
trajectories. The FedProx advantage is concentrated on minority
classes. The majority class (\textit{melanocytic nevi}, panel F) is
learned to F1 $\sim$0.88 by both algorithms within ten rounds, with no
visible separation thereafter---an expected consequence of the
partition design, in which every client holds nevi samples so no
inter-client drift exists on these parameters and the proximal anchor
has no effect to apply. By contrast, every class held by only two of
seven clients shows a sustained FedProx advantage. The largest
mid-training per-class gap occurs at round~50 on \textit{melanoma}
(panel~E), where FedAvg has barely learned the class
(F1~$\approx 0.06$) while FedProx has reached F1~$\approx 0.30$. This
single observation accounts for a substantial portion of the
mid-training macro-F1 gap. Per-class trajectories on minority classes
also exhibit wider $\pm$SEM bands than the majority class, reflecting
the higher variance of F1 estimates computed over the small number of
validation samples in those classes.

\section{Discussion}
%==============================================================================

The convergence curves in Figures~\ref{fig:curves-main} and
\ref{fig:curves-per-class} reveal a coherent mechanistic picture
consistent with the theoretical motivation for FedProx.

First, the training-loss panel of Figure~\ref{fig:curves-main} provides
direct evidence that the proximal regulariser is binding throughout
training. By round~150, FedAvg's mean training loss has decayed to
approximately~0.002---consistent with effective memorisation of each
client's local data---while FedProx's training loss is held more than
two-fold higher. This is not a side-effect of $\mu = 0.01$; it is the
defining property of the regularised objective. Under client
heterogeneity, when each client minimises an unregularised local
objective for $E = 20$ epochs, the resulting parameter vector drifts
toward the local minimum of that client's data slice. The FedAvg
size-weighted average of these drifted vectors is biased toward
whichever clients have the largest local data and the most aggressive
drift. FedProx's proximal anchor caps each client's drift magnitude,
preserving minority-class signals during aggregation.

Second, the per-class trajectories in Figure~\ref{fig:curves-per-class}
show that this drift-correction mechanism engages selectively. The
majority class \textit{melanocytic nevi}, held by every client, shows
no inter-algorithm difference at any round: when no client diverges
from the others on a class, the proximal term has nothing to constrain,
and the algorithms behave identically. By contrast, every minority
class---held by exactly two of seven clients in our partition---shows
a sustained FedProx advantage, with the largest mid-training gap on
\textit{melanoma}. This per-class asymmetry constitutes evidence that
FedProx's gain is not a generic, partition-blind perturbation but is
targeted at the classes where drift correction is needed.

Third, the mid-training communication-efficiency gain (3$\times$ fewer
rounds to reach val macro-F1 = 0.45) is consistent with FedProx's
theoretical convergence guarantee \citep{Li et al., 2020}. The
near-perfect identity of the two algorithms at round~1 confirms that
the paired-seed protocol has successfully equalised initial conditions,
so all subsequent divergence is attributable to the algorithmic
difference.
